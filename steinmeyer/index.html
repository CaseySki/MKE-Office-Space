The History

130 Years on Old World Third Street

From a wholesale grocer's cash-and-delivery empire to a landmarked home for modern offices, the Steinmeyer Building has anchored this corner of Westown through every era of Milwaukee's commercial life.

Photo placeholder — William Steinmeyer, founder (archival portrait)

1892 Loss & Legacy

A Founder Who Never Saw It Built

William Steinmeyer built the largest wholesale and retail grocery business in Milwaukee on a simple, disciplined model: order solicitors canvassed the city one day, cash-only deliveries followed the next. Steinmeyer died in 1892, a year before ground was broken on the building that would carry his name.

His son-in-law, Emil Ott, and his brother, Charles Steinmeyer, took over the firm and saw the building through to completion — and kept the Steinmeyer name over the door for another half-century.

Photo placeholder — Steinmeyer & Co. grocery warehouse interior, c. 1893

1893 The Founding

A Grocery Empire's New Home

Architects George B. Ferry and Alfred C. Clas design a five-story wholesale and retail grocery headquarters on Old World Third Street. The first phase — the entire Highland Avenue facade and the three north bays — rises in a simplified Romanesque Revival Commercial style, built at a cost of roughly $50,000.

Pressed red brick trimmed in orange terra cotta and wrought iron gives the facade its distinctive warmth — materials chosen for both permanence and presence on Milwaukee's most competitive retail corridor.

Photo placeholder — Old World 3rd St facade showing the 1898 addition

1898 The Expansion

The Southern Addition

Continued growth demands more space. Two additional bays are added to the south along Old World Third Street, seamlessly continuing Ferry & Clas's original design language. The expanded building now occupies the full corner lot, anchoring the intersection of Highland Avenue and Third Street.

A terra cotta plaque inscribed “STEINMEYER” still marks the second bay from the corner on Old World Third Street — the building's original main entrance.

Photo placeholder — storefront during the Steinmeyer Co. era, early 1900s

1940 End of an Era

The Grocery Business Closes

For nearly five decades, Emil Ott and Charles Steinmeyer carry on the firm's reputation for quality, service and fair pricing. But the rise of national grocery chains and the automobile — which freed shoppers from delivery routes and downtown warehouses alike — finally force the Steinmeyer Co. to close its doors in 1940.

Photo placeholder — bronze Milwaukee Landmark plaque, Highland Ave entrance

1974 Landmarked

A City Landmark, By Law

The City of Milwaukee Landmarks Commission designates the Steinmeyer Building a Milwaukee Landmark, recognizing its historical and architectural significance to the community. A bronze plaque is erected at the Highland Avenue entrance, permanently inscribing the building's legacy into the city's official record.

“Designed by G. B. Ferry and A. C. Clas, erected in 1893 and later for the wholesale and retail grocery firm founded by William Steinmeyer, and designated as a Milwaukee Landmark in 1974 in recognition of its historical and architectural significance to the community.”

Erected 1974 by the City of Milwaukee Landmarks Commission

Today The Renaissance

Heritage Meets the Modern Workplace

The ground floor has been thoughtfully adapted for retail while the upper stories have been converted into distinctive office space. Original high ceilings, exposed pressed brick and massive arched windows now frame contemporary workplaces that honor 130-plus years of continuous commercial purpose.

Listed on the National Register of Historic Places as part of the Old World Third Street Historic District, the Steinmeyer Building stands as proof that great architecture outlasts its era. Its next chapter is being written now.

The Architecture

The Ferry & Clas Masterwork

An example of simplified Romanesque Revival Commercial Style architecture, the Steinmeyer Building represents the confidence of a city at the height of its industrial boom — heavy masonry, rounded arches and deep-set windows built to last for centuries, not decades.

“The Steinmeyer Building is an example of simplified Romanesque Revival Commercial Style architecture. It was built in two phases… The first part was erected in 1893 and includes all of the Highland Avenue facade and the three north bays on Old World 3rd Street. The south two bays on Old World 3rd Street were added in 1898.”

The District

Old World Third Street

The Steinmeyer Building sits at the heart of one of Milwaukee's most historically dense neighborhoods — a walkable grid of landmarks charting the city's journey from fur-trading post to industrial powerhouse. It's part of the National Register's Old World Third Street Historic District, within the vibrant Westown neighborhood, in what regionally is Southeast Wisconsin and, more broadly, the Great Lakes and American Midwest.
